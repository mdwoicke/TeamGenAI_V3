How Companies Must Reskill, Repurpose, and Reinvent to Harness Generative AI

Read on

Date: August 3, 2024 3:03 PM

Gerard Gooch

As someone who deeply believes in the power and creativity of people, I find myself both excited and reflective as we stand at the threshold of a decade-long AI revolution. I am convinced that human ingenuity is unparalleled and that, when harnessed correctly, it will lead us into an era of unprecedented innovation. However, I am also acutely aware of the challenges this new era presents. The truth is that while Generative AI offers tremendous opportunities, it also poses significant risks. Some will thrive, embracing and fully leveraging the new possibilities AI presents, while others may struggle, potentially becoming disadvantaged as automation reshapes industries and displaces jobs.

This dichotomy—between the boundless potential of AI and the very real human cost of disruption—underpins my approach to the work we do at InteractiveGroup.ai and my work with YouthGPT. Our mission at InteractiveGroup.ai is to help businesses navigate this evolving landscape, not just by providing cutting-edge technology, but by ensuring that our solutions are human-centric, ethically sound, and designed to enhance rather than replace human capabilities. My mission at YouthGPT to empower youth with AI skills and job opportunities also reflects my personal and professional committment to positive change.

With almost 70% of top economic performers using proprietary software for differentiation and a third monetizing software directly, companies face a pressing need to master the rapidly evolving landscape of Generative AI (gen AI). This challenge involves navigating complex platforms, language models, and frameworks to deliver value-creating interactive AI workflows. The stakes are high: those who can quickly adapt to these new technologies will gain a significant competitive advantage.

However, while much of the focus remains on technology and tools, the broader impact on human resources (HR) and service delivery functions is often underestimated. As companies race to integrate gen AI, they must also manage existing operations—“keeping the lights on”—while simultaneously driving transformation. This dual responsibility will require more time and effort than many anticipate.

A critical challenge lies in repurposing existing talent, particularly software developers, creative professionals, and related roles. Current employees, who may be adept in traditional software development, often find themselves behind the curve in adopting generative AI skills. Meanwhile, the global supply of experienced gen AI talent remains tight. As a result, companies face the dual challenge of reducing headcount due to AI automation while also increasing their AI talent levels through hiring and reskilling.

For example, product managers (PMs) may need to evolve into roles such as "prompt engineers" or "Generative AI PM Specialists." With proper training, a PM with generative AI skills could potentially complete project-related tasks 40% faster, as demonstrated by productivity gains highlighted in the McKinsey report. IBM Software, for instance, saw a 30 to 40 percent increase in developer productivity when utilizing gen AI tools, underscoring the transformative potential of these technologies.

The McKinsey report identifies that integrating gen AI capabilities effectively requires companies to "rewire how they work," particularly in developing talent. However, focusing solely on generative AI will not suffice. Organizations must adopt a holistic approach encompassing Workforce Training, Organizational Change Management, Employee and Customer Experience, and People and Business Value Programs. Only by addressing these interconnected areas can companies fully capitalize on gen AI's potential while managing the complexities of transformation.

For HR and service delivery functions, the task is monumental. These departments must balance the immediate need to reskill and redeploy talent with the longer-term goal of creating a sustainable AI-ready workforce. This involves building apprenticeship models, as the McKinsey article suggests, and rethinking strategic workforce planning to focus on skills rather than rigid roles. A strategic shift toward skills-based talent management will allow organizations to be more agile and responsive to rapid technological advancements.

Top 3 Strategic Actions for HR Leaders

Implement Skills-Based Workforce Planning: Move away from traditional role-based planning and create a dynamic inventory of skills within the organization. Leverage AI tools to assess current skills, identify gaps, and prioritize reskilling efforts to align with strategic business goals.

Develop Comprehensive Apprenticeship Programs: Establish apprenticeship models where senior employees mentor less experienced colleagues. Ensure these programs are incentivized and supported with structured learning paths to develop both technical and soft skills necessary for AI-driven roles.

Enhance Change Management and Employee Experience Initiatives: Prepare the workforce for AI adoption by investing in change management programs that address resistance, build trust, and enhance the employee experience. Focus on transparent communication and provide continuous support to facilitate the transition to new roles and responsibilities.

Top 3 Strategic Actions for CTOs

Standardize AI Tools and Frameworks: Develop a unified AI strategy by standardizing the tools, models, and platforms used across the organization. This will help reduce complexity, streamline processes, and ensure consistent quality and risk management.

Invest in Scalable AI Talent Development Programs: Create targeted training programs to build AI capabilities at all levels. This includes investing in upskilling current staff and hiring specialized AI talent to fill critical gaps. Make use of low-code and no-code tools to democratize AI usage across the organization.

Integrate AI Risk Management into Development Processes: Develop clear guidelines and policies for managing AI-related risks. This includes training software teams to understand AI risks, use emerging testing tools, and integrate safeguards into their workflows. As AI technologies evolve, continuously update these policies to reflect the latest challenges and best practices.

By focusing on these strategic actions, HR leaders and CTOs can better prepare their organizations to navigate the AI revolution. Companies that prioritize a balanced approach to technology adoption, talent management, and organizational change will be well-positioned to harness the full potential of generative AI, turning uncertainty into opportunity.
